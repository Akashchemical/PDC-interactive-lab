Closed-loop transfer functions & controller law

Step A — Identify an FOPDT model

All classical tuning rules below assume the process has been fitted to a First-Order-Plus-Dead-Time model from a step test (the process reaction curve). Drag the model parameters:

Assumed model
Fitting tip (two-point method): from the recorded step response, read the times t₁ and t₂ at which y reaches 35.3 % and 85.3 % of its final change. Then τ = 0.67 (t₂ − t₁) and θ = 1.3 t₁ − 0.29 t₂. K = Δy∞ / Δu.

Step B — Apply a tuning rule

Every value updates live as you drag K, τ, θ. Press APPLY → to load the settings (and this FOPDT process) into the Closed-Loop tab and see the result.

Ziegler–Nichols — open loop (process reaction curve)

Cohen–Coon

Designed for ¼-decay ratio; works best when 0.1 < θ/τ < 1. Both ZN and Cohen–Coon give aggressive, oscillatory loops — common exam remark.

IMC / Direct Synthesis (λ-tuning)

λ (= τc) is the desired closed-loop time constant — the single knob trading speed against robustness. Rule of thumb: λ ≥ 0.8 θ; λ = θ (Skogestad) is a robust default, λ = τ is conservative.

Validity limits. The PRC rules need θ > 0 (they divide by θ), so θ is bounded below at 0.2 s here. They are derived for 0.1 ≲ θ/τ ≲ 1; outside that window expect poor performance — the live θ/τ readout below the sliders warns you.

Step C — Ziegler–Nichols closed-loop (ultimate gain)

Found by raising a P-only controller until the loop oscillates with constant amplitude. Here Ku and Pu are computed exactly from the frequency response: the phase crossover where ∠Gp = −180°.

Process reaction curve (unit step in u)

Dashed markers: dead time θ, and the 63.2 % point reached at t = θ + τ. This is the curve every open-loop tuning rule reads its K, τ and θ from.

Stability margins

Design guidelines: GM ≥ 1.7 (≈ 4.6 dB) and PM ≥ 30°. Margins below these mean the loop is fragile to model error.

Vertical dashed lines mark the gain crossover ωg (|G| = 1, i.e. 0 dB) and the phase crossover ωc (∠G = −180°).

What the Bode plot tells you

Bode stability criterion: the closed loop is stable if |GOL| < 1 at the frequency where ∠GOL = −180° (the phase crossover ωc). Dead time adds phase lag −ωθ that grows without limit — that is why delay destabilises loops.

With a perfect model the characteristic equation becomes 1 + Gc·G̃p = 0 — the dead time drops out of the stability problem, so you can tune much harder. Drag the model-mismatch slider to see the famous sensitivity of the Smith predictor to delay error.

D · Ratio control — hold the ratio of two flows

Ratio law

Classic applications: air-to-fuel ratio in burners, reflux ratio in distillation, blending. The wild stream FA is measured but not manipulated; its measurement is multiplied by the desired ratio R to generate the setpoint of a flow controller on the manipulated stream FB. It is really a special case of feedforward. Related strategies you should be able to name: override/selective control (a high/low selector picks which controller drives the valve, used for constraint protection), split-range control (one controller output drives two valves over different ranges, e.g. heating 0–50 %, cooling 50–100 %), and gain scheduling / adaptive control (controller settings change with operating point to handle nonlinearity).

Assumptions used everywhere in this lab — so you can quote them in an exam: (1) all variables are deviation variables about a steady state, so every response starts at zero; (2) models are linear time-invariant transfer functions; (3) measurement and valve dynamics are lumped into Gp (Gm = Gv = 1); (4) load disturbances enter at the process input, i.e. Gd = Gp, unless a separate Gd is shown (feedforward section); (5) the PID is the ideal parallel form with derivative on the filtered measurement (N = 10) and no output saturation, so linear theory applies exactly; (6) integration is 4th-order Runge–Kutta with Δt ≤ τ_min/40, and dead time is an exact sample delay; (7) settling time uses a ±5 % band; (8) slider ranges are bounded on purpose — see the limits note in tab 01.
